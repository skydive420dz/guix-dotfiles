#+date: 2026-07-16

* Outcome and boundary

P2.K1a is a read-only attribution review of Phase 0 findings P0-F01 through
P0-F03.  It changes no System or Home declaration, package, kernel, initrd,
boot parameter, module, firmware, runtime service, or machine state.  It also
does not authorize a reboot.

The evidence supports these conclusions:

- P0-F01 is not evidence that PID 1, Guile, or Shepherd crashed.  The exact
  signal is attributable to BDWGC's intentional, handled address-limit probe
  while the Guix initrd's static Guile was PID 1.  This finding is closed for
  the recorded signature.
- P0-F02 is a real, concentrated IRQ 7 storm followed by the kernel's
  defensive disable path.  The originating interrupt source and whether the
  cause belongs to firmware, routing, hardware, or the driver remain unknown.
- P0-F03 is a real disagreement between TSC and HPET.  Linux continued
  timekeeping through its automatic selection of HPET.  The evidence does not
  prove which clock or subsystem was wrong, and no material performance cost
  has been measured.
- The IRQ storm precedes the TSC watchdog warning by about one second, but no
  causal relationship is established.
- No mitigation is justified by the current evidence.

P0-F04 and P0-F05 are outside P2.K1a.  Their wireless regulatory-database and
WPA-marker work remain deferred and require a separately named slice.

* P0-F01 -- early =init[1]= Guile signal

** Observed facts

- Both inspected boots emitted the exact fault address =0x3fff00= and
  instruction pointer =0x4d5593=.
- The initrd's static Guile executable begins at =0x400000= with executable
  text at =0x401000=.  Its text size matches the kernel's =401000+202000=
  report, and =0x4d5593= lies inside it.
- The address =0x3fff00= is exactly one 256-byte scan step below the first
  mapping.
- The runtime Guile 3.0.9 launcher used by Shepherd is only 14,512 bytes with a
  =0x255=-byte text segment; =0x4d5593= cannot belong to it.
- Shepherd loaded its configuration immediately afterward, retained PID 1,
  and remained healthy with five threads.
- No PID 1 exit, kernel panic, service loss, repeated post-startup fault, or
  user-visible failure was observed.

** Exact attribution

Static disassembly around =0x4d5593= installs =SIGSEGV= and =SIGBUS= handlers,
uses =sigsetjmp=, walks memory downward in =0x100= increments, reads the
candidate address, then restores the handlers after the boundary fault.  The
identical installed libgc 8.2.8 sequence resolves through its debug symbols to
=GC_find_limit_with_bound= at =os_dep.c:1029=.

BDWGC's =GC_find_limit= deliberately probes an address-space boundary and
handles the resulting signal.  Linux's global-init signal-reporting special
case explains why the handled signal is nevertheless printed as
=init[1]: segfault=.  The log line therefore does not describe process death.

Classification: =ATTRIBUTED / CLOSED=.  For this exact signature, “PID 1
crashed,” “Guile crashed,” and “Shepherd crashed” are incorrect descriptions.

Primary sources:

- [[https://github.com/ivmai/bdwgc/blob/v8.2.8/os_dep.c#L454-L470][BDWGC warning about its intentional SIGSEGV probe]];
- [[https://github.com/ivmai/bdwgc/blob/v8.2.8/os_dep.c#L954-L1034][BDWGC address-limit probing implementation]];
- [[https://github.com/gregkh/linux/blob/v7.0.14/arch/x86/mm/fault.c][Linux 7.0.14 x86 fault reporting]];
- [[https://github.com/gregkh/linux/blob/v7.0.14/kernel/signal.c][Linux global-init signal handling]];
- [[https://guix.gnu.org/manual/devel/en/html_node/Initial-RAM-Disk.html][Guix initial-RAM-disk architecture]].

** Impact and reopen conditions

No adverse impact is established, and no Guile, Shepherd, kernel, or boot
change is required.  The attribution applies only to the exact recorded
address, IP, executable mapping, and early-init path.  Reopen P0-F01 if PID 1
exits, Shepherd fails to load, the kernel panics, the fault repeats after early
startup, the signature changes, or a service failure is tied to the event.

* P0-F02 -- IRQ 7 storm and defensive disable

** Observed facts

- Both inspected boots reached a live IRQ 7 count of 100,000 before Linux
  printed =irq 7: nobody cared= and disabled IRQ 7.
- The first retained stack names =amd_gpio_irq_handler=; the live registration
  identifies =pinctrl_amd=.
- Current =/sys/kernel/irq/7/= data records action =pinctrl_amd=, hardware IRQ
  7, chip =IR-IO-APIC=, type =level=, name =fasteoi=, and per-CPU counts
  =0,0,0,100000=.
- =CONFIG_PINCTRL_AMD=y=: this driver is built into the kernel, so absence from
  =lsmod= or =modinfo= is expected.
- The driver owns platform device =AMDI0030:00=, ACPI HID =AMDI0030=, path
  =\\_SB_.GPIO=, and a 256-line GPIO chip.
- Core boot, networking, desktop, Bluetooth, and audio acceptance subsequently
  passed.

** Attribution boundary

Linux disables an IRQ after its spurious-interrupt accounting observes roughly
99,900 unhandled interrupts among 100,000 closely spaced interrupts.  The
100,000 counter is the threshold observation; it is not proof that every one
of 100,000 deliveries was unhandled.

The AMD GPIO driver uses a shared parent IRQ.  Its handler returns handled only
when it finds a pending, unmasked GPIO with a valid Linux IRQ mapping.  The
narrow defensible inference is:

#+begin_quote
The AMD GPIO parent IRQ repeatedly fired, but the registered handler could not
associate the deliveries with a valid claimed GPIO source, so Linux disabled
the parent IRQ defensively.
#+end_quote

This does not establish that =pinctrl_amd= generated the electrical interrupt
or that the driver itself is defective.

Primary sources:

- [[https://github.com/gregkh/linux/blob/v7.0.14/kernel/irq/spurious.c#L143-L178][Linux spurious-IRQ reporting and disable path]];
- [[https://github.com/gregkh/linux/blob/v7.0.14/kernel/irq/spurious.c#L320-L419][Linux unhandled-interrupt accounting]];
- [[https://github.com/gregkh/linux/blob/v7.0.14/drivers/pinctrl/pinctrl-amd.c#L608-L691][AMD GPIO interrupt handler]];
- [[https://github.com/gregkh/linux/blob/v7.0.14/drivers/pinctrl/pinctrl-amd.c#L1160-L1251][AMD GPIO parent-IRQ registration]];
- [[https://docs.kernel.org/PCI/boot-interrupts.html][Linux =irqpoll= documentation]].

** Unknowns and impact

Remaining cause classes are a firmware-enabled or asserted unclaimed GPIO,
incorrect ACPI routing or GPIO status, a physically asserted line, IRQ/SCI
sharing, or a Linux 7.0.14 driver regression.  Upstream AMD GPIO changes show
that these classes exist; none is an established fix for this machine.

The kernel stopped the storm, but disabling the parent IRQ may also suppress a
real button, wake source, sensor, or other platform event.  Successful daily
desktop checks do not prove that every GPIO consumer remains functional.
P0-F02 remains =OPEN / IMPACT UNKNOWN=.

* P0-F03 -- TSC/HPET watchdog disagreement

** Observed facts

- Both inspected boots initially selected TSC and rejected it within seconds.
- Boot A differed by 871,760 ns over 479,983,210 ns, about 1,816 ppm.
- Boot B differed by 876,317 ns over 475,133,152 ns, about 1,844 ppm.
- Linux marked TSC unstable and selected HPET.  The current clocksource is
  =hpet= and the available list is =hpet acpi_pm=; TSC is no longer eligible.
- No kernel-command-line override selected this state.
- The Ryzen 3 2200G advertises =constant_tsc= and =nonstop_tsc=.

** Attribution boundary

The clocksource watchdog detected disagreement beyond its allowance and
invoked the normal unstable-clocksource path.  This proves relative
disagreement between TSC and HPET.  It does not identify which clock or
intervening platform behavior was wrong.

The kernel's “most likely due to broken BIOS” sentence is generic source text,
not a machine-specific diagnosis.  The CPU flags argue against ordinary
P-state frequency changes, but do not guarantee cross-core synchronization,
HPET correctness, or freedom from firmware/SMI interference.

Primary sources:

- [[https://github.com/gregkh/linux/blob/v7.0.14/kernel/time/clocksource.c#L110-L150][Linux clocksource-watchdog thresholds]];
- [[https://github.com/gregkh/linux/blob/v7.0.14/kernel/time/clocksource.c#L470-L540][Linux watchdog comparison and unstable decision]];
- [[https://github.com/gregkh/linux/blob/v7.0.14/arch/x86/kernel/tsc.c#L1141-L1229][x86 TSC unstable handling]];
- [[https://github.com/gregkh/linux/blob/v7.0.14/kernel/sched/clock.c#L144-L178][generic broken-BIOS warning source]];
- [[https://www.amd.com/content/dam/amd/en/documents/processor-tech-docs/programmer-references/56255_OSRR.pdf][AMD invariant-TSC documentation]].

** Unknowns and impact

Remaining cause classes are TSC synchronization or a platform defect, an
HPET/reference-clock error, firmware or SMI interference, kernel calibration
or watchdog behavior, or another hardware timing fault.  The roughly
one-second ordering after P0-F02 is correlation only.

Linux continued timekeeping through the automatic HPET fallback.  That does
not establish HPET as the correct side.  A performance cost is possible but
has not been isolated, and no disruptive normal-use behavior is currently
attributed to it.  P0-F03 remains =OPEN / IMPACT UNMEASURED=, not an
authorization to force either clocksource.

- =/proc/interrupts= and =/proc/irq/7/spurious=;

- MCE, EDAC, RAS, thermal, and other hardware-error messages.

Preserve the external raw file privately and verify its digest.  Review it for
serials, UUIDs, MAC addresses, command-line details, firmware-table contents,
and other sensitive identifiers.  Only a separately redacted derivative may
be considered for =docs/audits/data/=, with its own digest and an explicit
sanitization label; repository retention is not automatic.

** G2 -- controlled cold-boot recurrence

Choose the replication count before rebooting.  Each run must record a new
boot ID and the complete G1 artifact.  Do not count repeated probes, P2.1, or
P2.2 on one boot as additional recurrence.

** G3 -- genuine comparison candidate

Before claiming a kernel comparison:

- select one justified prior or alternate maintained kernel;
- build it without activation;
- verify hardware closure, firmware, initrd, boot parameters, and recovery;
- retain current System 87 and an accepted rollback generation;
- define exact pass, fail, and rollback conditions;
- obtain explicit authorization before activation and cold reboot.

** G4 -- at most one reversible experiment

Only after G1 through G3 may a later slice select one hypothesis.  Source
comparison, build, activation, reboot, runtime verification, and physical hand
tests remain separate gates.

* No-change and stop conditions

P2.K1a authorizes none of the following:

- =irqpoll=;
- ~tsc=unstable~ or another clocksource override;
- disabling HPET, the watchdog, or TSC checks;
- blacklisting, unbinding, masking, or writing state for =pinctrl_amd=;
- ACPI overrides or debugfs writes;
- BIOS setting changes or firmware flashing;
- booting ancient unaccepted generations as an assumed safe comparison;
- custom-kernel or =amdgpu= initrd work;
- treating P0-F04 or P0-F05 as implicitly included.

Stop if evidence capture requires a mutation rather than a read, live identity
drifts during capture, a comparison lacks verified rollback, firmware
compatibility cannot be established for the exact machine, or a new GPIO,
clock, thermal, storage, kernel, or PID 1 failure appears.
